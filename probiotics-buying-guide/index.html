How to choose a probiotics

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Look for a probiotic that lists specific, well-researched strains (not just a species name), has a colony-forming unit (CFU) count that matches your needs, and is stored and delivered in a way that keeps the bacteria alive. For women, pay extra attention to strains like Lactobacillus rhamnosus and Lactobacillus reuteri, which are often studied for vaginal and urinary health.

What strains should I look for in a probiotic?

Not all probiotics are the same. If the label only says “Lactobacillus acidophilus” without a specific strain name, you’re getting a generic blend. Reputable brands list the full strain, like Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG or Bifidobacterium lactis BB-12. For women’s health, strains such as Lactobacillus rhamnosus GR-1 and Lactobacillus reuteri RC-14 have been widely studied for supporting vaginal flora. A multi-strain formula can be beneficial because each strain has a different job. One might support digestion, another immune function. But more isn’t always better, focus on the strains that have published research backing their benefits. Brands like Culturelle and Jarrow Formulas clearly name their strains and cite the science behind them.

How many CFUs do I really need?

CFU (colony-forming units) is the count of live bacteria per dose. Many people assume higher is better, but that’s not always true. Some strains are effective at just a few billion CFUs, while others may need 10 or 20 billion to make a difference. For general gut health, a dose between 5 and 15 billion CFUs is a common starting point. If you’re taking a probiotic to address a specific condition like antibiotic-related diarrhea or vaginal infections, a higher count might be recommended. But your body won’t necessarily absorb all those bacteria, some just pass through. It’s more important to choose a product with a guarantee of potency through the expiration date, not just the time of manufacture.

Capsules, powders, or gummies: which form works best?

Capsules and powders generally survive stomach acid better than gummies because the form protects the bacteria. However, some gummy probiotics use shelf-stable strains and can be a convenient option if you dislike swallowing pills. Powders can be sprinkled into cold food or drink, but heat can kill the bacteria, so avoid hot liquids. For women on the go, single-serving powder sticks or small capsules are easy to pack. If you have a sensitive stomach, a delayed-release capsule (sometimes labeled “acid-resistant”) may help more bacteria reach the intestines. Brands like Garden of Life offer both powdered and capsule forms with clear instructions on storage.

Should a women’s probiotic contain prebiotics?

Prebiotics are fibers that feed the good bacteria. Some probiotic supplements combine them into one product (called a synbiotic). This can be convenient, but it’s not necessary for everyone. If you already eat a diet rich in fiber, think oats, bananas, onions, and garlic, you may not need a prebiotic in your pill. Be aware that adding prebiotics can cause gas or bloating in some people, especially if your gut isn’t used to fermentable fibers. If you’re sensitive, choose a probiotic without added prebiotics and get your fiber from food instead.

Do I need to refrigerate my probiotic?

Not all probiotics require refrigeration. Many brands now offer shelf-stable varieties that are freeze-dried and packaged to survive room temperature. Check the label: if it says “store in a cool, dry place,” that’s usually fine. But if it says “refrigerate after opening,” don’t skip it.Heat and moisture are the enemies of live bacteria. A probiotic that’s left in a hot car or bathroom cabinet can lose potency fast. For women who travel frequently, shelf-stable options like those from Renew Life or Culturelle are more practical. Always look for a statement that verifies the CFU count at the end of the product’s shelf life, not just at the time of manufacture.

How do I know if a probiotic is right for women’s health?

Women’s probiotics often include strains that specifically support vaginal and urinary tract health. Look for Lactobacillus rhamnosus GR-1 and Lactobacillus reuteri RC-14, two of the most clinically studied strains for these purposes. Some products also contain Lactobacillus crispatus or Lactobacillus gasseri, which are naturally dominant in a healthy vaginal microbiome. Beyond the label, check that the brand manufactures in a facility that follows good manufacturing practices (GMP) and that the product has been tested by a third party. Consumer reviews can also be helpful: many women report fewer yeast infections or less bloating after switching to a targeted formula. Just remember that results vary from person to person.

Frequently asked questions

Can I take probiotics while on antibiotics?

Yes, but timing matters. Take the probiotic at least two to three hours apart from the antibiotic so the antibiotic doesn’t kill the beneficial bacteria you just swallowed. Some people find that starting the probiotic a day or two after the antibiotic course begins helps reduce digestive upset.

How long does it take for a probiotic to work?

It depends on what you’re taking it for. Some people notice less bloating or more regular bowel movements within a week. For immune support or vaginal health, it may take several weeks of consistent use to see a difference. Patience and consistency are key.

Should I take my probiotic with food or on an empty stomach?

Most experts recommend taking a probiotic with a meal or shortly after eating. Food helps protect the bacteria from stomach acid. A study on Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains found that taking them with a meal that contains some fat improved survival through the digestive tract.

Can I get enough probiotics from food instead of supplements?

Fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, and kimchi provide beneficial bacteria, but the strains and counts vary widely. For a targeted effect, like a specific strain shown to support women’s health, a supplement gives you a consistent, science-backed dose. Food is a great addition, but not always a reliable substitute.

What’s the difference between a probiotic and a prebiotic?

Probiotics are live bacteria that directly add to the community of good microbes in your gut. Prebiotics are non-digestible fibers that feed those bacteria, helping them grow. You can get prebiotics from foods like garlic, onions, and bananas, or from a supplement. Some products combine both.

Are probiotics safe for pregnant or nursing women?

Most common probiotic strains are considered safe during pregnancy and breastfeeding, but you should always talk to your doctor first. Some strains, particularly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium, have been studied in pregnant women and shown no adverse effects. Still, because supplements aren’t tightly regulated, it’s best to choose a well-known brand with third-party testing.

In shortChoosing a probiotic doesn’t have to be overwhelming. Focus on specific strains backed by research, pick a CFU count that matches your reason for taking it, and pay attention to how it’s stored and delivered. For women, strains like Lactobacillus rhamnosus GR-1 and Lactobacillus reuteri RC-14 are worth seeking out. Start with one that fits your lifestyle, give it at least a few weeks, and listen to your body, it’ll let you know if the choice was right.
